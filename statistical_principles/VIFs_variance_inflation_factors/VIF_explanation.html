Variance Inflation Factors (VIF)

This page summarizes concepts and formulas related to variance inflation factors (VIF), intercept VIF, and contrast VIF (cVIF).

Definition

The variance inflation factor for predictor j is

\[ VIF_j = \frac{1}{1 - R_j^2} \]

where \(R_j^2\) comes from regressing predictor j on the remaining predictors.

Interpretation

  • VIF = 1 : orthogonal predictors
  • VIF 1–3 : low collinearity
  • VIF 3–5 : moderate collinearity
  • VIF > 10 : problematic multicollinearity

Variance Perspective

Coefficient variance:

\[ Var(\hat{\beta}_j) = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1}_{jj} \]

Intercept VIF

The intercept also has a VIF measuring how well the constant column can be predicted by regressors.

\[ VIF_0 = \frac{1}{1 - R_0^2} \]

Large intercept VIF indicates unstable coefficient estimates.

Mean Centering

Centering predictors makes them orthogonal to the intercept and stabilizes the model.

Contrast VIF (cVIF)

For contrast vector c:

\[ cVIF = \frac{c (X^T X)^{-1} c'}{c (X^T X \circ I)^{-1} c'} \]

This measures variance inflation for contrasts of coefficients.

Example

Dummy-coded regressors:

\[ 1 = D_1 + D_2 + D_3 + D_4 \]

This causes perfect collinearity but contrasts between conditions remain estimable.
